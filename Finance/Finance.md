# Core Financial and Regulatory Concepts: A Business Overview

## 1. CCAR (Comprehensive Capital Analysis and Review)
* **Definition:** A regulatory framework introduced by the U.S. Federal Reserve to evaluate the capital planning processes and capital adequacy of large financial institutions.
* **Core Purpose:** It functions as a massive "stress test." The goal is to ensure that banks have enough capital on hand to survive severe, hypothetical economic crises (like a massive spike in unemployment or a housing market crash) without failing or requiring a taxpayer bailout.
* **Business Impact:** If a bank fails CCAR, the Federal Reserve can restrict its ability to pay dividends to shareholders or buy back its own stock.

## 2. Transfer Pricing (Funds Transfer Pricing - FTP)
* **Definition:** An internal financial management mechanism used by banks to allocate interest income and expenses across different business units.
* **Core Purpose:** It centralizes the management of interest rate risk and liquidity into the bank's corporate treasury.
* **How It Works:** It separates the bank into "fund providers" (like retail branches that collect deposits) and "fund users" (like a commercial lending desk). The treasury "pays" the retail branch for the deposits they bring in and "charges" the lending desk for the cash they use to issue loans. This ensures each unit's profitability is measured fairly based strictly on their core activities.

## 3. FX (Foreign Exchange)
* **Definition:** The marketplace for exchanging one national currency for another, as well as the corporate accounting process of currency translation.
* **Core Purpose:** In a global economy, multinational corporations and banks must deal in various currencies. FX involves both the active trading of these currencies to facilitate international business and the hedging strategies used to protect against unfavorable shifts in exchange rates.
* **Accounting Impact:** For reporting purposes, a global entity must translate the financial results of its foreign subsidiaries (e.g., revenues in Euros, Yen, or Pounds) into a single "functional" home currency (like USD) to present a consolidated financial statement.

## 4. Positions
* **Definition:** The exact amount of a specific security, commodity, currency, or liability held by an institution or individual at a specific point in time.
* **Core Purpose:** It represents an entity's exact financial exposure or ownership stake in a particular asset.
* **Types of Positions:**
    * **Long Position:** You own the asset outright and expect its value to rise.
    * **Short Position:** You have borrowed the asset and sold it, hoping the price will drop so you can buy it back cheaper and pocket the difference.

## 5. IRR (Interest Rate Risk & Internal Rate of Return)
*Note: Depending on the specific financial context, IRR generally refers to one of two distinct concepts.*
* **Interest Rate Risk (IRR):** The danger that a bank's profitability or overall capital value will decline due to adverse movements in market interest rates. For example, if a bank is locked into receiving 3% on 30-year fixed mortgages, but market interest rates rise and they suddenly have to pay depositors 4% to keep their cash, the bank loses money.
* **Internal Rate of Return (IRR):** A corporate finance metric used to evaluate the attractiveness of a potential investment or project. It is the annualized effective compounded return rate that makes the net present value (NPV) of all cash flows (both positive and negative) from a particular investment equal to zero. Simply put, it is the expected annual growth rate of an investment.
